How armed conflicts disrupt a global fight to end child mortality

Explore

In 1989, the United Nations’ Convention on the Rights of a Child (UNCRC) declared all children have the right to ‘life, survival and development’, and be ‘protected from violence, abuse or neglect’.

Since the UNCRC’s ratification, the global child mortality rate continues to decline, as governments work to deliver intervention measures to stave off causes of preventable death.

Driven by rapid global economic growth, billions have also been pulled out of extreme poverty and live with better access to basic needs, particularly clean water, food and health care.

With a better standard of living, more children grow up in healthier and safer environments.

But when you look at the data closely, some parts of the world have experienced disruptions, deviating at some point, in their journey to end child mortality.

These regions have one clear thing in common - they have been affected by internal armed conflicts, which has generated some of the largest modern day refugee populations.

Let’s zoom in a little bit, to one such region - Sub-Saharan Africa.

According to data by the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR), this is the refugee population by country of origin from 1990, to the most recent data, that is, 2019.

Although the total refugee population in Sub-Saharan Africa has never dropped below 2.5 million people, there are two key displacement events in time - once in 1994, and the other, today.

First, let's have a look at what happened in 1994.

Post World War II saw decades of independence movements and resistance against European powers finally come to an end in Sub-Saharan Africa, as dozens of nations declared independence.

However, colonial resistance was replaced with civil strife, which led to the displacement of millions of innocent civilians, including children.

Although armed conflicts were also ongoing in other parts of the region, the following key events caused refugee numbers to skyrocket.

1977 - 1992 Mozambican Civil WAr

Armed conflict between Marxist and anti-communist forces within Mozambique finally came to an end in 1992. In this same year, the refugee population out of Mozambique peaked, displacing over 1.5 million people.

‎1993 ethnic violence in Burundi and 1994 genocide in Rwanda

The 1993 and 1994 genocides against the Tutsi and some of the Hutu ethnic groups in Burundi and Rwanda were the culmination of decades of inequality and colonial divisions between the ethnic groups. Over 3 million refugees fled their home country in what was known as the Great Lakes Refugee Crisis.

1993 Burundi Civil War

Following the genocidal events, civil war broke out in Burundi, further displacing hundreds of thousands of innocent civilians.

From these events, and many like them, villages became warzones and innocent civilians were forced to leave behind the life they knew.

But why was child mortality specifically impacted by this?

A child living in Sub-Saharan Africa in 1994 was most at risk of preventable water-borne and infectious diseases. They already had a statistically lower chance of survival past the age of five, compared to children living in non-conflict zones around the world.

But as a refugee, children are exposed to further risks, particularly:

  • Deprivation of basic needs
  • Separation from parents or family members
  • Gender-based violence and abuse
  • Exploitation

The consequence? As a result of the events in the early 1990s, child mortality deviated from the common downward trajectory and climbed by as much as 8 deaths per 1000 live births.

In Sub-Saharan Africa, this represented an additional 180 000 children annually, who never lived to see their fifth birthday.

The events in 1994 caused a mass exodus of innocent people, many of whom were children. If we revisit our data it looks as though history is repeating itself.

Today, several countries in Sub-Saharan Africa contine to face similar threats from armed conflicts between rebel forces, terrorist groups and militant groups, and are known to be some of the most dangerous places to be in the world, to grow up as a child (Save our Children, 2021)

Ongoing South Sudanese Civil War

Since the start of its civil war in 2013, South Sudan has seen more than 2.4 million refugees flee to neighbouring countries, ‘the majority of whom are women and children’. (Mercy Corps, 2020)

Ongoing armed conflicts

Somalia, the Democratic Republic of Congo and many more nations continue to experience civil unrest, as a result of dissonance between terrorist groups, rebel forces, militias and government forces. These conflicts represent millions more displacements.

The ongoing armed conflicts of today, are dire reflections of past events. We have already seen that civil wars and mass displacement hurt children the most.
